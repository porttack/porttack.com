I'm here to report on last week's showing of "Screenagers Next Chapter" to about 40 parents and students.

For context, I first stood before your predecessors in 2016. Let me read the abbreviated NYT quote I read in 2016:

> In an interview with Steve Jobs when the iPad was just out: "So, your kids must love the iPad?" ... "They haven't used it," he told me. "We limit how much technology our kids use at home." ...

> Since then, I've met a number of ... executives ... who say similar things: they strictly limit their children's screen time...

> I was perplexed by this parenting style. After all, most parents seem to take the opposite approach, letting their children bathe in the glow of [screens] ... day and night....

> Chris Anderson, [for example] ... has instituted time limits and parental controls on every device in his home. "My kids accuse me and my wife of being fascists and overly concerned about tech, and they say that none of their friends have the same rules," ... "That's because we have seen the dangers of technology firsthand. I've seen it in myself, I don't want to see that happen to my kids."

> The dangers to kids he is referring to [are many] ... perhaps worst of all, becoming addicted to their devices, just like their parents.

<!--more-->

Last week I showed the movie to 100 students during school and again to parents in the evening. I want to report out what I heard from students and parents in discussions after the movie.

I asked the evening parents and students whether high school, middle school, and even sixth graders should see the film. The response across all age groups was overwhelmingly positive — they believe all students should see it. Interestingly, multiple parents even asked how to get cell phones banned at school, and one mentioned the new book called "The Anxious Generation" which explores the same issues in more depth. I read it this weekend and strongly recommend it.

The showing to seventh graders received mixed reviews. Collectively the responses were that the movie was meh, the movie was better than no-movie, and the content wasn't important. Interestingly, one seventh grader who struggles on multiple levels vocally decried all her classmates as liars and backed up her statement with her lived experience. I think many of the students did benefit, and none wanted to admit the content was useful for any number of reasons -- peer pressure, denial, fear, etc.

In my classroom, there is a clear correlation between student performance and self-assessed screen health. Healthier relationships with screens = higher performance.

<img src="/docs/assets/images/screen_health_vs_performance.png" alt="Scatter plot: Healthy Score vs. Good Student — showing correlation between screen health and academic performance" style="max-width: 100%; display: block; margin: 0.8em auto;">

I encourage our community of educators and students to watch the movie -- and to do it during school if possible.

*Delivered 5/1/2024*
