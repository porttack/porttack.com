Every so often I get strong students who run out of computer science to take. They've worked through the sequence, they want more, and there isn't a next course for them. When that happens, the conversation usually turns to options that carry college credit: self-study the AP Computer Science A exam, or take a course like CMU's CPCS for transfer units. These are capable students who can handle either. And it keeps coming up as more of them push past the end of our course list.

Here's where I landed, and I'll say the punchline first. The credit is the floor, not the building. Pick whichever's easiest to clear, because the part that actually matters isn't on the transcript at all.

<!--more-->

## The two options, honestly

**AP CSA** is the portable floor. It isn't college enrollment. It's a standardized exam every registrar in the country already knows how to read, so it never trips the "we don't accept credit earned in high school" rule that catches a lot of families off guard. The tradeoff is that it's capped at roughly one course, and it rides entirely on exam day. For a self-study student that means no instructor scaffolding and no safety net, so a 3 and the credit is gone. The upside is that CSA is one of the more self-study-friendly exams out there: tight curriculum, endless free prep, real sample tests.

**CMU CPCS** is the higher-ceiling, higher-uncertainty option. I use a lot of CMU material in my classes depending on the course, and it's genuinely good content for the right students. But Carnegie Mellon is a private university in Pennsylvania, and credit from an out-of-state private, earned while a student is still in high school, is exactly the kind a registrar scrutinizes hardest. Plenty of schools won't grant it at all, no matter how strong the institution, and that includes the California publics. There's no guarantee a UC or CSU accepts these units, and I wouldn't promise a student they will. The brand doesn't settle it. The registrar isn't questioning the quality, just the circumstance. I've been told CMU will call and advocate to get the credits accepted wherever a student lands. Maybe. Advocacy can tip a discretionary call, but it can't override a hard policy, and a verbal promise is the weakest version of one. The fact that decides the whole thing is a simple one: are those units genuine transferable college credit, or institutional units that only count at CMU? Until you know, their value anywhere off that campus is a question mark.

## Now here's the part that actually matters

Step back and ask what either option is really buying. The answer is the same for both: academic CS1 in a different wrapper. Syntax, objects, recursion, autograded problems in a sandbox. That's it. That's all it is.

And there is nothing wrong with that, until you mistake it for something it isn't. Neither one builds real-world experience, because that was never their job. There's no version control. No collaboration. No ambiguous spec, no stakeholder who changes their mind halfway through, no system that fails at 11 p.m. the night before a deadline and has to be debugged with tired hands. Asking an autograded intro course to produce an engineer is a category error. It produces a credit. The credit is the floor, not the building.

This actually resolves the agonizing rather than deepening it. Credit and experience are different things. The credit is table stakes: necessary, fungible, and a little hollow as a signal, because every applicant has some version of it. The experience is the differentiator, and that's the part that's genuinely hard to come by.

It's also the part we already do. Building ROVs. The makerspace. A MATE competition season with real engineering documentation, a budget, a team, and a deadline that doesn't move. Mock tech interviews. High schoolers teaching elementary kids at science night. That's the work colleges and employers actually notice, not because it's on a transcript but because it's hard to fake and harder to get. No exam mints it. No platform autogrades it.

## So spend the least on the part that matters least

If a student wants the credit, the calculus is simple: bank whichever one clears cheapest. For most that's AP CSA, low friction and universally legible. The CMU course only earns the extra effort if it's buying something the exam can't, and "might advocate, verbally" isn't that. Either way, the credit is the easy part.

The thing I'd actually point them toward has nothing to do with a transcript. It's the same line I keep drawing in these posts: the fundamentals are the floor, not the finish. Produce knowledge, not just output.

And here's the piece that's specific to what I do. I'm a CTE teacher. A student can self-study an exam and pass it alone in a corner. But that's the smallest thing they could do with the year, and it skips the real opportunity sitting right in front of them: building something that matters under the mentorship of someone who spent a career as an engineer. The exam they can get on their own. That, they can't.

So get the credit if you want it. But this work isn't something you study for. You learn it by doing: you build something, it breaks, and you figure out why. Come build something that breaks.
