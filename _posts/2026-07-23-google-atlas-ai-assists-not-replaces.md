[Google released the first findings from its AI & Economy ATLAS today](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/research/understanding-the-ai-economy/), built from roughly 15 million de-identified Gemini conversations spanning more than 150 countries, 140 languages, and 800 occupations. The headline finding cuts directly against the "AI is coming for your job" narrative:

<!--more-->

> The average worker uses AI on only about 21% of their tasks, and fewer than 10% of work-related interactions aim at fully automating cognitive work.

Gemini shows up in roughly 68% of occupations, covering 90% of U.S. employment - so the reach is wide. But reach isn't depth. Most people are using it the way you'd use a sharp colleague: for research, drafting, iteration, troubleshooting, and learning, not for handing over the whole job. [As Axios reported](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/23/google-ai-adoption-work-atlas), that broad-but-shallow pattern held even in physical, hands-on occupations - auto technicians and industrial mechanics lean on AI for diagnostics and troubleshooting at rates that rival how knowledge workers use it for writing.

This lands as a direct counterweight to [the NPR teacher poll I wrote about in June](/2026/06/05/teachers-ai-impact-poll.html), where 54% of teachers said AI is making critical thinking harder to teach. Both things can be true - real anxiety about atrophying skills, and data showing most actual use is collaborative rather than automating - but it's worth separating the fear from the evidence when a student asks whether a skill like coding is still worth learning. Right now the evidence says: yes, because most of the economy is still using AI as a tool a skilled person wields on a fraction of their tasks, not a replacement for the person doing them.
