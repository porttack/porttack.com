An economics professor at Brown gave a take-home midterm this spring. The class averaged 96. Forty of eighty-six students earned a perfect score. If you have ever graded a stack of exams, you know that number is not a triumph. It is a smoke alarm.

The professor is Roberto Serrano, who has taught Welfare Economics and Social Choice Theory for nearly two decades. His midterm had never averaged anything close to 96; the usual range was 65 to 80. After a December shooting on campus, he moved the exam to a take-home format to ease the stress of testing in a room. Then the perfect scores rolled in. He fed his own questions into ChatGPT and watched it produce answers that matched what students had turned in, down to some of the same odd phrasings.

What he did next is the interesting part. He did not run to the honor council. He told the class he suspected AI use and moved the final to an in-person room. Then he gave them a dare: if the final matched the midterm, the midterm would stand; if not, he would throw it out. **Prove me wrong.**

They could not. As [reported by Inside Higher Ed](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty/learning-assessment/2026/07/08/brown-professor-suspects-most-his-class-used-ai-cheat), the in-person final averaged 48.6, the lowest in the history of the course. Of the twenty-seven students who dropped or skipped it, twenty-two had scored a perfect 100 on the midterm. The strongest students on paper disappeared the moment they had to think without a copilot.

This is the cost I keep coming back to. I have written before about [what happens when AI removes the productive struggle](/2026/02/04/when-ai-removes-productive-struggle.html) that learning actually requires. That is the whole point: the struggle, the effortful reach for an answer you do not yet have, is not in the way of learning. It is how the learning happens. Skip it and there is nothing underneath. Serrano's class is that idea at scale. The take-home did not measure economics. It measured who was willing to offload the thinking. A study out of MIT last year found the same shape in the lab: students who leaned on a chatbot to write showed the weakest brain engagement and drifted toward copy and paste, building what the researchers called cognitive debt. (That study was a preprint, and its authors rightly warned against the "brain rot" headlines. The honest claim is narrower and more useful: skip the hard reps early and the skill does not form.)

Here is the other side, and it matters. AI is not the villain of this story. For a student who already owns the fundamentals, the same tool that let Serrano's class cheat can multiply how much they learn: faster feedback, harder questions, a tutor that never gets tired. That is exactly why I have argued we need [two AI policies, not one](/2026/06/03/protect-struggle-multiply-learning.html): protect the struggle for students still building the basics, and multiply the learning for students ready to go further. Serrano's midterm is what it looks like when we protect nothing.

So the lesson is not "ban the machine." It is narrower and harder. Assessment that can be finished by pressing a button now measures your willingness to press it, and nothing more.

The students did what the incentives rewarded, which makes this less a story about a broken generation and more a challenge to what, how, and why we educate our citizens. Serrano changed one thing, and the gap revealed itself. The rest of us have to face a much larger, uncomfortable reality: there is no playbook for this yet. We are in entirely uncharted territory - educational, ethical, and human. Finding our way through will require us to rethink not just how we test, but how we want human society to work.
