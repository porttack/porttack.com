On Friday, February 27, the Pentagon labeled Anthropic — one of America's leading AI companies — a "supply chain risk." That's a tag normally reserved for hostile foreign adversaries. Anthropic's offense? Its CEO had refused to sign a military contract unless it kept two lines: no mass domestic surveillance, no fully autonomous weapons. Federal agencies were ordered to stop using its AI. That same night, a competitor signed its own Pentagon deal — and was rewarded for saying yes.

One company blacklisted for saying no. Another cheered for saying yes.

So I put a question to my high school students. Can a company have a conscience? But I'm not only asking them. I'm asking you.

<!--more-->

Here's how the weekend actually unfolded.

On Thursday, February 26, Anthropic's Dario Amodei published two red lines his company had asked to keep in its Pentagon contract:

- No mass domestic surveillance
- No fully autonomous weapons

He could not, in his words, "in good conscience" agree to a contract without those safeguards. That same morning, OpenAI's Sam Altman told his employees he stood with Anthropic.

By Friday, the story had flipped. Federal agencies were ordered to stop using Anthropic's AI. The Pentagon declared the company a "supply chain risk." And that night — barely 24 hours after saying he stood with Anthropic — Altman announced that OpenAI had signed its own deal with the Pentagon, with the very same red lines Dario had just been punished for defending.

This isn't the first time. Dario saw it coming, and he [said so himself](https://darioamodei.com/essay/the-adolescence-of-technology). In Anthropic's early days, he handed new hires a copy of "The Making of the Atomic Bomb." His message: if you build something powerful enough, the powers that be will come for it. Oppenheimer built the bomb, then spent his life trying to limit its use — and was stripped of his security clearance and his influence. Dario drew his line and was declared a security risk.

Reasonable people disagree about whether a private company should be able to override a national security decision. That's exactly why I put the debate in front of my students.

Then the public answered the question they were asking. By Sunday, Claude hit #1 on the App Store for the first time ever. #QuitGPT exploded across social media. Millions canceled ChatGPT and switched to the company that said no. A pop star with millions of followers publicly sided with Anthropic — over ethics, not features.

Consumers voted with their wallets. A company that chose conscience over compliance didn't just survive. It was cheered by the very people it was trying to protect.

My students watch all of this and draw conclusions. They wonder what's happening to the world around them, and which jobs will even exist when they enter the workforce. When the system punishes the company that stands up and rewards the one that falls in line, the natural question is: why bother having principles at all?

That's not hypothetical. That's a teenager trying to figure out the rules.

If you work in education, you know this question echoes in our hallways. Every teacher has heard it: Why should I do the work if I can just get AI to write my essay for me?

Same question. If the system rewards the shortcut, why take the hard road?

I don't think we answer that with rules and punishment. I think we answer it with stories like this, where someone drew a line, paid a real price, and the world showed up for them anyway.

This is how we show students that character counts.
