Can a company have a conscience?

That's the question I put to my high school students after the events of late February. But I'm not just asking them. I'm asking you.

And another question: Why compare Dario to Oppenheimer?

<!--more-->

My students wonder what's happening to the world around them. Which jobs will exist when they enter the workforce? They watch the adults in charge and draw conclusions.

Our system punished a company for standing on principle and rewarded a competitor who claims to have won a contract with the same principles. Certainly confusing. What's really going on? If the system rewards those who fall in line and punishes those who stand up, why bother having principles at all?

That's not hypothetical. That's a teenager trying to figure out the rules.

On Thursday, February 26, Anthropic's Dario Amodei published two red lines his company had requested previously in their contract with the Pentagon:

- No mass domestic surveillance
- No fully autonomous weapons

He could not "in good conscience" agree to a contract without these safeguards. The same day, OpenAI's Sam Altman messaged employees that he stands with Anthropic.

By Friday, federal agencies were ordered to stop using Anthropic's AI. The Pentagon declared Anthropic a "supply chain risk," a label normally reserved for hostile foreign companies. That night, Altman announced OpenAI signed a deal with the Pentagon with the same red lines Dario was punished for.

One company blacklisted for saying no. Another rewarded for saying yes.

About the Oppenheimer comparison: Dario made it himself. In Anthropic's early days, Dario gave new hires copies of "The Making of the Atomic Bomb." If you build something powerful enough, the powers that be will come for it. Oppenheimer built the bomb; afterward, he spent his life trying to limit its use and was stripped of his influence. Dario drew his line and was declared a security risk.

Reasonable people disagree about whether a private company should override national security decisions. That's why I put the debate in front of my students.

Then the public answered the question my students were asking. By Sunday, Claude hit #1 on the App Store. #QuitGPT exploded. Millions canceled ChatGPT and switched to the company that said no. Employees across Google and OpenAI signed an open letter in solidarity.

Consumers voted with their wallets. A company that chose conscience over compliance didn't just survive. It was cheered by the people it was trying to protect.

If you work in education, you know this question echoes in our hallways. Every teacher has heard it: Why should I do the work if I can just get AI to write my essay for me?

Same question. If the system rewards the shortcut, why take the hard road?

I don't think we answer that with rules and punishment. I think we answer it with stories like this, where someone drew a line, paid a real price, and the world showed up for them anyway.

This is how we show students that character counts.
