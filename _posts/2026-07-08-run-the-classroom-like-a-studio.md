## Opening: back to the cheating question

- Part 1 opened with the fight over detecting AI cheating and called it the wrong fight. This post is the alternative in practice.
- **Key line:** You don't detect cheating in an artifact. You verify understanding live.
- Frame: after twenty-five years in software, I stopped trying to invent classroom systems and started importing the ones companies already use. Students aren't playing school; they're running a company that builds underwater robots.

## The studio

- The classroom is a studio run like an engineering company. Structure exists to protect building time, not replace it.
- Design-first culture: the season starts with planning and evidence (an autopsy of last year's competition scores), and building follows from requirements, not enthusiasm.
- Everything below is a real industry practice, imported with as little translation as possible. That's the point: the class *is* the work-based learning the standards ask for.

## Sprints (agile, borrowed honestly)

- Three-week sprints. Teams of three to seven publicly commit to deliverables at sprint planning; one owner per deliverable; estimates doubled, because everyone underestimates.
- Every sprint ends at the pool. "Done" means demonstrated in the water. The demo day cannot be argued with, extended, or emailed about.
- **Key line:** The water is the project manager.
- Consequences do the teaching: a missed handoff surfaces at the pool, not in a gradebook comment.

## Decision records (ADRs for teenagers)

- Software teams keep Architecture Decision Records; my students keep Design Decision Records: title, context, options, decision, why, consequences. Half a page.
- Rule from industry: whoever makes the decision writes the record, in the same sprint.
- At sprint review, a random team member defends a random record. That is judging rehearsal, interview rehearsal, and accountability in one move -- and it makes documentation impossible to pawn off on one student who transcribes words without owning ideas.
- The documentation lead assembles the tech doc from team-authored records; they do not invent content.

## Socratic seminars and structured debate

- How engineers actually argue about tradeoffs, taught as a form: seminars on real mission science (deep-sea mining, ocean acidification, ghost fishing gear), with assigned random sides.
- Requirements before solutions: some seminars exist to rehearse writing a design spec, not to reach a verdict.

## The 90-second oral check (the semi-AI-proof part)

- Written prompts are preparation; the oral check or live modification is the verification. Every written response can be audited with one live follow-up drawn from its own content.
- Four prompt types that resist AI by construction:
  - Prediction before measurement (you commit before the data exists)
  - Local evidence (our pool day, our dead parts, named classmates in seminar)
  - Honest questions (what you still don't understand, specifically)
  - Artifact-anchored (explain the thing on the bench in front of you)
- Teach-back to your team counts as an alternate clearing. If you can teach it, you have it.
- **This closes Part 1's loop:** the answer to AI cheating was never a better detector. It's assessment where the student's own understanding is the only thing that works.

## Timecards and sweat equity

- Hours logged like a timecard, adapted from Tom Sachs' studio code (*Ten Bullets*). "That record belongs to you."
- On-task habit made visible; the studio as sacred space; thoroughness counts.

## Procurement discipline (the boring skill that runs the world)

- Bill of materials due one sprint ahead: exact part, link, quantity, cost, need-by date, one-sentence purpose. Vague requests bounce the same day.
- A student supply-chain manager owns intake and tracking. Slow school purchasing stops being an excuse and starts being a constraint you engineer around, like the real world.

## Grading: complete or "not yet"

- Work is complete or not yet; redos always open. Students keep their A by keeping lesson cards cleared and contributions visible.
- Norms co-created with students on day one. Rigor comes from the oral checks and the water, not from point deductions.
- Tie back to Part 2: every practice above maps onto the CTE anchor standards -- teamwork (9.0), responsibility and flexibility (7.0), problem solving (5.0), portfolio and demonstration (11.0). The standards alignment isn't an afterthought; it's the defensibility.

## The caveat that keeps it honest

- Any classroom system that costs more than minutes a day dies. The todo lists died. The exit tickets died. Everything above survived because the friction is near zero and the consequences are real.
- This is a labor of dedication, not a turnkey kit. But none of it required permission from anyone: the standards (Part 2) already ask for exactly this.

## Close

- Every practice in this post is something employers already do. The class is not preparation for a first job; it is the first job.
- We are done optimizing for the wrong skills. This is what optimizing for the right ones looks like on a Tuesday.
