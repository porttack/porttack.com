If you've only half-followed this one, the whiplash is real: two years ago Apple put ChatGPT inside Siri. This week Apple filed a 41-page lawsuit accusing OpenAI of running a scheme to steal its trade secrets "at every level."

<!--more-->

Here's the chain of events, in order.

**2024:** Apple and OpenAI announce a partnership - ChatGPT gets built into iOS as an option inside Siri. Everyone treats them as allies.

**2025:** OpenAI buys io Products, the hardware startup founded by Tang Tan, a 24-year Apple veteran and former VP of product design, working alongside legendary former Apple design chief Jony Ive. Price tag: $6.4 billion. The stated goal is a new category of AI hardware to sit alongside, or maybe replace, the smartphone. That is not a subtle move on Apple's turf.

**This month:** Apple's new Siri quietly starts running on a Google Gemini model - not OpenAI's - in a deal reportedly worth $1 billion a year. The same week, Apple files suit against OpenAI, Tan, and a second former Apple engineer named Chang Liu, alleging a "pattern of theft" of confidential hardware information: main-logic-board manufacturing details, supplier relationships, and unreleased product specs. [Per Fortune, the complaint claims Tan used confidential Apple codenames while recruiting](https://fortune.com/2026/07/10/apple-openai-lawsuit-trade-secrets-theft-allegations/) and told candidates to bring actual Apple parts to interviews "for show and tell." Liu allegedly kept an Apple laptop after leaving, exploited an authentication bug on it, and downloaded dozens of confidential files months after his last day.

OpenAI's response so far: "We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets."

So the confusion is fair - this reads less like a breakup and more like Apple watched a partner turn into a competitor in real time, and responded by pulling the plug (Gemini replaces ChatGPT in Siri) and going to court on the same week. [Bloomberg frames it as the opening round of a much bigger fight](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/how-apple-s-lawsuit-threatens-to-disrupt-openai-s-bid-to-rival-the-iphone) over who gets to build the device that comes after the iPhone. One name conspicuously missing from the complaint is Ive himself - reporting suggests Apple simply doesn't have evidence tying him personally to the alleged theft, even though his fingerprints are all over OpenAI's hardware ambitions.

What makes this useful beyond the gossip is the legal shape of it. This isn't a patent fight over an invention - it's a trade secrets case, which turns on a much messier question: did specific people take specific confidential information they only had access to because of their old job, and use it somewhere new? That's the same question that comes up anytime an employee leaves one company for a competitor, just scaled to two of the most valuable companies on earth. It's worth sitting with if you're a student who'll someday sign an NDA and wonder what you're actually allowed to remember when you walk out the door.

It's also another data point in the same pattern I wrote about with [Anthropic and the Pentagon](/2026/03/18/can-a-company-have-a-conscience.html): the AI industry's alliances are far less stable than the press releases make them sound. Today's platform partner is next year's lawsuit.

---

**For the classroom:** if you're using this alongside copyright and patents, the contrast worth drawing is what makes trade secrets different from both. Patents and copyright are registered or fixed in a tangible form, disclosed publicly, and protected for a fixed term no matter how someone else arrives at the same thing - independently inventing a patented device still infringes it. Trade secrets are never disclosed and never registered, protected for as long as secrecy holds, but infringement requires *improper means*. Reverse-engineering or independently figuring out the same manufacturing process is legal.

That's the question worth putting to students: is OpenAI in trouble because it built competing hardware, or only because of *how* Apple says the information got there? The specific acts in the complaint - a kept laptop, an exploited login bug, recruiters allegedly asking candidates to bring physical parts to interviews - are the "how." Take those away and there's no case, no matter how similar the products end up looking.
