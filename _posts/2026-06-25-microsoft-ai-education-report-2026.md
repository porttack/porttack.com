[Microsoft released the third edition of its annual AI in Education Report](https://news.microsoft.com/source/2026/06/24/microsofts-new-ai-in-education-report-highlights-widespread-adoption-and-increasing-demand-for-support/) ahead of this summer's ISTE conference, and the two numbers that matter sit right next to each other:

<!--more-->

> 92% of students and education leaders - and 88% of educators - have already used AI for school. Yet 77% of students say they've had no formal training on it.

Adoption without instruction is the whole problem in one stat. 42% of educators and 41% of students name academic integrity as a top worry, which tracks with everything else I've written about this year - but the more interesting number is the appetite on the other side of the gap: 66% of educators and 52% of students say they want training on a monthly or quarterly basis. Kids and teachers aren't resisting structure here, they're asking for it and not getting it.

Microsoft's Matt Jubelirer, General Manager of Education Marketing, put it this way: "Educators around the world are embracing AI as a classroom ally, and they're now asking not if, but how to make the most of it."

That 92%-vs-77% gap is basically the argument for teaching this stuff explicitly instead of assuming a usage policy stands in for instruction. It's the other half of [the NPR teacher poll I covered in June](/2026/06/05/teachers-ai-impact-poll.html): teachers are worried about what AI does to critical thinking, and here's the population most exposed to that risk, using the tool constantly with almost nobody teaching them how to use it well.
