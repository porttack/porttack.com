When Ring aired its Super Bowl ad, a Seattle artist named Maggie Butler [filmed herself pulling her doorbell camera off her porch and flipping it off](https://www.centraloregondaily.com/news/consumer/ring-ai-dog-search-privacy-backlash/article_377dd8f6-73e8-4606-aac3-5c3921bdc0bd.html). The clip was watched millions of times. She had bought the camera to catch package thieves, but the ad had convinced her the thing on her wall had quietly become something else. "They aren't just tracking lost dogs," she said, "they're tracking you and your neighbors."

The ad itself was gentle. A lost dog, a crying kid, a neighborhood pulling together, a golden retriever trotting home. Ring paid for one of the most expensive thirty seconds in television to make you feel exactly that. So it is worth asking why Butler and others went outside to pull their cameras down instead, why senators and Republican strategists attacked the ad in the same breath, and why the company quietly killed a police-tech partnership four days later.

Because the ad was not really about a dog.

<!--more-->

## What the ad was actually selling

The feature is called **Search Party**. Here is how it works. You report your lost dog in the Ring app, and Ring's AI scans footage from *other people's* Ring cameras nearby, looking for a visual match. If a neighbor's camera thinks it spotted your dog, that neighbor gets a notification and can choose to share the clip.

Read that again with the emphasis where it belongs. The system searches your neighbors' cameras. And those neighbors did not sign up. [Search Party is on by default](https://www.digitaltrends.com/home/rings-search-party-super-bowl-ad-sold-pet-love-and-surveillance-fears-heres-how-to-opt-out/) for Ring cameras that save to the cloud. It is opt-out, not opt-in. To turn it off you have to know it exists, go digging in the app, and switch it off camera by camera, a process a member of Congress later described as [six steps deep](https://krishnamoorthi.house.gov/media/press-releases/krishnamoorthi-raises-alarm-over-rings-new-ai-search-party-feature-citing).

So the ad was not really introducing a dog finder. It was quietly announcing that millions of privately owned cameras had been networked into a single searchable system, and that most of the people who own those cameras never agreed to it in any meaningful way. Ring already sells facial recognition for people you know, a feature called Familiar Faces, and a tool that lets police request footage from a neighborhood. Search Party is the AI that ties the field of view together.

## The neighborhood reacts

The backlash was fast and, unusually, bipartisan. Democratic Senator Ed Markey warned that Ring was turning neighborhoods into surveillance networks. A Republican strategist called the ad flatly dystopian. The account WeRateDogs, millions of followers and not exactly a privacy activist, described it as turning private homes into surveillance outposts. A parody ad went viral in which the five-star reviews came from the CIA, FBI, and ICE. The Electronic Frontier Foundation called it a surveillance nightmare. USA Today ran a piece headlined "Why are people disconnecting or destroying their Ring cameras?" There is no sign of a mass exodus, but Butler's video was one of many.

Then a tell. Four days after the ad, [Ring canceled a partnership](https://www.aol.com/articles/amazon-rings-search-party-feature-142500683.html) with Flock Safety, a company that makes the automated license-plate readers used by police. Companies do not unwind partnerships that fast over a feature they are proud of. They do it when the quiet part gets loud.

## The concern was never really about dogs

Critics kept naming the same worry, and it has a name worth teaching: **function creep**, when a technology built and sold for one purpose gradually gets used for another. If an AI can find a specific golden retriever in a neighborhood, the same system can find a specific person. Dogs, after all, do not commit crimes.

In February, [404 Media reported on an internal email](https://www.404media.co/leaked-email-suggests-ring-plans-to-expand-search-party-surveillance-beyond-dogs/) in which Ring's founder described Search Party as a foundation built "first for finding dogs." Ring, for its part, [says](https://www.techradar.com/home/home-security/ring-says-its-leaked-plan-to-zero-out-crime-in-neighborhoods-doesnt-mean-mass-surveillance) the feature is purpose-built for pets and does not process human biometrics or track people. You can decide how reassuring you find that. But you do not have to rely on a leaked email to see where this is pointed, because the founder has been saying it out loud for years.

## The founder has said this for a decade

Jamie Siminoff did not stumble into surveillance. He built Ring around it on purpose, and he has never hidden the goal.

Siminoff founded the company in 2013 after famously failing to land a deal on Shark Tank, and he insists his title is not CEO but chief inventor. From the beginning, Ring's stated mission has been the same three words: reduce crime in neighborhoods. He stepped away in 2023, then [returned in 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/30/ring-founder-ai-amazon-doorbell-police.html) explicitly to restore that original mission, now with far more AI at his disposal. Ahead of a new book, he told The Verge that he believed Ring could [almost zero out crime](https://petapixel.com/2025/10/23/ring-ceo-claims-ai-powered-cameras-can-eliminate-most-crime/) in some neighborhoods within a year or two.

That reframes the whole story. The lost dog was never the point. It was the friendliest possible on-ramp to a mission Siminoff has pursued for over a decade: the densest possible camera coverage in a neighborhood, paired with AI, aimed at crime. Whether that is a promise or a warning depends on where you sit, and that is precisely the argument worth having. It is worth noting that the crime-reduction claims are contested. Amazon has cited an old Los Angeles pilot suggesting Ring cut burglaries by more than half, and multiple outlets have disputed whether the cameras reduce crime at all. Privacy advocates have long warned that the Neighbors app encourages racial profiling and turns residents into informants.

## Why I teach it

California decided this belongs in school. In 2023, AB 873 added Education Code section 33548, folding media literacy and digital citizenship into the state's curriculum frameworks across core subjects, kindergarten through twelfth grade. In 2024, AB 2876 amended that same section to add artificial intelligence literacy. The state's own language defines media literacy as the ability to access, analyze, evaluate, and use media. That is not a slogan. That is a job description for exactly this lesson.

An abstract lecture on surveillance slides right off a twelve-year-old. A Super Bowl commercial about a lost dog does not. The Ring ad hands students a real, current, emotionally engineered piece of media and asks them to do the one thing the ad was designed to prevent: slow down and notice the puppy standing in front of the surveillance network. They learn to separate how a message makes them feel from what it actually does. They learn that "on by default" is a decision someone made about their consent. They learn that the friendliest use case is often the on-ramp to something bigger.

The best thing I can teach a student is not to be afraid of a doorbell. It is to see the puppy in the ad and ask what it is standing in front of. That is a skill for a classroom. It is also a skill for a neighborhood.
