I've promised my students I'd read this for over a month now. I'm going to go fast…

We've gone from the industrial age to the information age. And we're on the cusp of what will either be the AI age or the human age. I'm voting for the human age; where humans benefit from AI and are always in the loop. It's coming fast one way or the other.

<!--more-->

Last time I was here, I talked about what happens when AI removes the productive struggle students need to learn. I stand by that. But I left something out, and I learned how important that omission was just recently, as my students dug into more advanced work this spring.

My students design and build robots and compete at a level where the technical demands are significant: embedded systems, hardware-software integration, real engineering documentation. At that level, AI shows up the way it does in industry: not just in the code, but in building interfaces, drafting documentation, thinking through design decisions. Some companies now track whether engineers are using AI enough, and flag those who aren't.

The concern I raised in February is real: intro students who use AI to bypass the cognitive struggle of learning fundamentals don't retain the fundamentals. The only thing they retain is the cognitive struggle required to prompt an AI tutor into maybe producing something credible enough for me to assess positively. But that's a different situation from an experienced student using AI to reach further: more complex designs, more professional output, higher-level ideation, at moments where the tool extends what they can do rather than replacing what they should be doing themselves.

Teaching them how to prompt well, how to evaluate what comes back, how to slow down, avoid the slop, and produce professional results. That's the skill.

Dr. Rumman Chowdhury talks about keeping the human in the loop, and has said we should teach students to produce knowledge, not just output. I agree. For advanced students, AI is a multiplier that allows them to produce significantly more knowledge than they otherwise could. I want to help students do this by being the teacher in the loop.

I don't know exactly what it looks like in practice, but I'm ready to experiment with caution and safety in mind. I think doing so supports my more advanced students.
